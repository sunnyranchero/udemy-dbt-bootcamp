Honestly, I thought the course was well structured. I don't expect the course to answer every quirk needed to get to the answer but this one seemed to provide enough info to do so. It was challenging in more than the intended way. It covered creating keys for key pair auth, an opportunity for version control practice (I tend to put all of my course material in a private repo), navigating the terminal, practice with vs code and its many extensions (in my case, troubleshooting 2 extensions fighting themselves), and much more. 

I do wish there was more detail about setting up the environment manually instead of using the tool. It's understandable why that is the default  considering many people could be coming from different backgrounds. 

I'm personally coming from an accounting background and transitioned into data engineering about 2 years ago. After using legacy tools at my company, there were 2 contractors that came in to implement an airflow, dbt, and snowflake tech stack for our reporting source. Considering we were coming from DBMoto/Syniti, SQL Server, and an outdated SAP BODS/Data Integrator... it was eye opening. Most of the stuff you covered in the course really did make it easier to understand what the contractors were doing and why. I'm not a stranger to coding either, in accounting I forced myself to learn vba, python, powershell and eventually some bash. I tell you this stuff to help maybe give you an idea of the amount of help your course provides. I'm currently working on a project at work that will go through the entire pipeline: DB2/jde > DBMoto > SQL Server > Airflow/DBT/Snowflake. I can tell you right now, the DBT section makes a lot more sense right.
